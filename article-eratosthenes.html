The Mathematics

Now comes the really clever part—math! Eratosthenes knew that the 7.2-degree angle was part of the total 360-degree circle around the Earth. Think of it like a pizza: If you cut out a slice that is 7.2 degrees, how much of the pizza is left?

By using simple math, Eratosthenes figured out that the distance between Alexandria and Syene must be about 1/50th of the way around the Earth. So, if you imagine that the Earth is like a giant circle, the distance between the two cities was one tiny slice of it.

Next, Eratosthenes used the known distance between Alexandria and Syene—about 800 kilometers (or 500 miles)—to figure out how big the whole Earth must be. If 800 kilometers was 1/50th of the Earth's circumference (the distance all the way around), then multiplying 800 by 50 gives us 40,000 kilometers (about 24,860 miles) for the Earth's total circumference!

How Close Was Eratosthenes?

Surprisingly, Eratosthenes' calculation was really close! The actual circumference of the Earth is around 40,075 kilometers (24,901 miles). His measurement was only off by a tiny bit! This means that even though he didn't have the technology we have today, he was able to come up with a remarkably accurate answer using just math, basic tools, and a lot of curiosity.

Eratosthenes' experiment was amazing because it was one of the first times anyone tried to figure out the size of the Earth in such a scientific way. Before this, many people thought the Earth was flat or that it was much smaller than it really is. His work helped people understand that the Earth is round and that it's much larger than anyone had imagined.

Not only did Eratosthenes' experiment help teach us about the size of our planet, but it also showed how powerful curiosity and clever thinking can be! By just measuring shadows and using simple math, he was able to unlock a huge mystery.

What Can You Learn from Eratosthenes

So, what can you take away from Eratosthenes' experiment? Well, it shows that even when something seems impossible or huge, you can figure it out by breaking it down into smaller pieces. Whether it's measuring the Earth or solving a tricky problem, thinking carefully and using what you already know can help you find the answer.
